Entrepreneurship and Negotiation

What is an entrepreneur ?

Entrepreneurship seeks to understand how opportunities to create something new arise and are discovered or created by specific persons who then use various mears to exploit or develop them, thus producing a wide range of effects.

Key Activites:

  • Identifying an opportunity
  • Exploiting or developing this opportunity
  • Runnig a new business successfully

Process of entrepreneurship:

  • Recognition of an opportunity
  • Deciding to proceed and assembling resources
  • Launching a new venture
  • Building success
  • Hurvesting the rewards

Summary of key points

  • As a field of busines seeks to understand how opportunities arise and how individuals develop them into procucing a wide range of effects.
  • In recent years, the allure has increased so much that more people are choosing it as a career.
  • As a branch of business has roots in many other discriplines - economics, sociology, behavioural science.
  • Is a process.
  • Happens when there is a «collision» at the crossroads of valuable opportunities and enterprising individuals.

Not all opportunities are equals. Some are more favorable. For exemple, an opportunity that needs a lot of money to initalise may not be adapted for a new company with limited budget. Opportunities exists because people do not have the same informations.

Improving a life-style, being innovative, trying new way of doing things is a key to entrepreneurship.

Changes

Deregulations can open a new market as well as regulations introducing new constraints. Being the first one to adapt to this regulation can be very profitable. Some regulations and political decisions can help new companies and thus make it easier to try catching opportunities.

Social and demographic changes are important as well as it alters demand for products and services. For exemple an aging population will open opportunities in a lot of facilities targetting older poeple.

New vs Established firms

New firms are usually the ones that introduce new products and services, and established firms are usually the ones that introduce new production processes.

It is way more easy to copy a product or a service than a production process.

New firms do better in:

  • Industries that have greater R&D intensity
  • Industrise in which public sector organisations produce most of the technology
  • Industries in which small firms are the better innovators
  • Larger markets
  • Rapidly growing markets
  • More heavily segmented markets
  • When Industries are young
  • Before a dominant design emerges
  • Competence destroying change
  • Discrete products and services
  • Ideas embedded in human capital

New firms performs more poorly in:

  • Capital-intensive industries
  • Advertising-intensive industries
  • Concentrated industries
  • Industries composed of mostly large firms

Advantages of Established firms

  • The learning curve
  • Established reputation
  • Positive cash flow
  • Economies of scale
  • Complementary assets
